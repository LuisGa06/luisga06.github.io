About the Chow Chow dog

The distinctive-looking Chow Chow dog breed has a proud, independent spirit that some describe as catlike. They can be aloof — if you’re looking for a cuddle buddy, this probably isn’t the best breed for you — and downright suspicious of strangers. But for the right person, they can be a fiercely loyal companion

Origin and history

Breef origin

A breed of dog that differs from most others in having a blue-black tongue. The breed originated in China and is known to date to the Han dynasty (206 BC–AD 220); it is believed by some to be one of the oldest of all breeds and is genetically very close to the wolf. The name chow chow is apparently derived from an English term used in the 19th century to refer to cargo from the Orient.

History of the Chow Chow

The Chow Chow as it is known today, is represented in the pottery and sculptures of the Han Chinese Dynasty (206 AC to 22 AC); other sources indicate that it was still a much more old race and can have come originally from the Arctic Circle, then migrated into Mongolia, Siberia, and China.

With the ending of the Tang dynasty came an increasing national poverty and, except within the monasteries, selective breeding ceased. The original pure Chow breed only stayed in the houses of the richest merchants, some noble, and in the monasteries.

Diet of the Chow Chow

Chow Chows are very chubby dogs, they eat a lot. It is recommended to give only small amounts of meat, otherwise if you give the dog big amounts of meat it would cause damage to the skin and coat

Take care with the food

Chow Chows are often categorized as overweight dogs. They’re also prone to diabetes since they’re not the most active. Therefore, be mindful not to overfeed pups, avoid giving them table scraps and implement proper exercise. Whether you opt for dry or wet dog food, pay attention to your Chow’s skin for any signs of allergy or irritation, since certain foods can trigger allergic reactions or digestive problems. If you have any particular health concerns about your dog’s diet and nutrition – including weight, dietary restrictions, or even deciphering what ‘human’ foods are safe to feed your Chow – speak with your veterinarian. They can provide you with professional advice and recommendations on the nutrients your Chow Chow needs, whether he’s a pup or a senior dog.
